Population Aging in the Netherlands vs Population Density

Introduction

Population aging has become one of the most significant demographic shifts of the 21st century. Many in Europe, including the Netherlands, are experiencing rising proportions of elderly due to declining birth rates and the increasing life expectancy. As someone who has studied and lived in Korea, a country that has the worst aging crisis in the world, this topic holds personal relevance and importance. Understanding how aging varies across regions can help us identify where we need to pay close attention to issues that are creating from an aging population, like social, economic, and healthcare challenges.

In this study, I compare Dutch provinces to analyze which regions are aging the most and, in particular, test whether provinces with higher population density tend to have younger or older populations. Density may be an important factor because urban areas have a reputation for younger, student-filled population while rural areas have been known to have aging population with a shrinking labor force. Thus, my research question is: How is population density related to the share of elderly residents (65+) across Dutch provinces?

Background

The theory behind population aging is when the amount of elderly increases compared to younger populations. This is phenomenon is occurring globally, specifically in Western European and East Asian countries, and it will continue to increase in the future decades (UN DESA, 2022). South Korea is a country that is at the forefront of this problem and it is illustrating the problems that can arise from it like social and economic pressures (OECD 2023). The Netherlands is also encountering a rise in an aging population (see graphing), and it plays an important role in demographic structure. Urban regions with a high density can attract younger populations. So, rural regions may lost their younger populations for these reasons. For this reason, testing the relationship between population density and aging can reveal whether an urban-rural divide can explain possible aging patterns in the Netherlands.

Cleaning and merging the data

This just cleans and standardizes the population dataset by renaming the columns and converting all percentage values into numeric format so they can be analyzed and mapped correctly.

Here we need to fix the provincial name variables and join

p_pop_clean <- p_pop |>
  rename(
    provincienaam = Provincienaam,
    pct_0_15 = `% 0 tot 15 jaar`,
    pct_15_25 = `% 15 tot 25 jaar`,
    pct_25_45 = `% 25 tot 45 jaar`,
    pct_45_65 = `% 45 tot 65 jaar`,
    pct_65_plus = `% 65 jaar of ouder`
  ) |>
  mutate(
    across(
      c(pct_0_15, pct_15_25, pct_25_45, pct_45_65, pct_65_plus),
      ~ readr::parse_number(.x)
    ),
    provincienaam = if_else(
      provincienaam == "Frysln (Friesland)",
      "Friesland",
      provincienaam
    )
  ) |>
  # add density
  left_join(density_df, by = "provincienaam") |>
  # this will be our join key
  mutate(prov_join = provincienaam)

p_map_clean <- p_map |>
  mutate(
    prov_join = case_when(
      prov_name %in% c("Frysln", "Fryslan") ~ "Friesland",
      TRUE ~ as.character(prov_name)
    )
  )

# join and reproject
nl_map <- p_map_clean |>

Analysis:

From this model we can see that the percentage of elderly residents in the Netherlands is systematically related to how densely populated a province is. The regression without Flevoland shows a clear negative slope: as population density increases, the share of residents aged 65+ tends to decrease, and this relationship is statistically significant (p 0.014). This suggests that more rural, less dense provinces carry a heavier aging burden, while the denser areas are relatively younger on average.

ANOVA Model Analysis

The ANOVA model shows that the elderly population differs greatly across the different Dutch density groups; this is evident from the low p-value (p = 0.038), which means the aging pattern is not spatially random. Provinces in the low-density group have much higher percentages of elderly residents compared to the high-density group. When we account for Flevoland separately, we see that it behaves as a special case: it is very dense but still relatively young, which weakens the overall trend when included, and strengthens it when excluded. The residual map also highlights other aging areas like Zeeland, Limburg, and Drenthe, which age faster than expected even after accounting for density.

Conclusions

What did I find?

The results show that population aging in the Netherlands is unevenly distributed. Some provinces experience much stronger aging pressures compared to others. When we look at these spatial difference, we can see that particularly in the southern regions they may be || start facing pressures on their healthcare, labor, and services.

How did it differ from what I expected?

The results show that population aging in the Netherlands is unevenly distributed across space and clearly linked to population density. Some provinces experience much stronger aging pressures compared to others, especially the low-density southern and eastern regions. When we look at these spatial differences, we can see that these areas may start facing pressures on their healthcare, labor, and services, while dense, newer areas like Flevoland remain relatively young and stand out as spatial outliers in the model.

Were the results significant?

Yes, the results were statistically significant. The ANOVA test returned a p-value of 0.038, which means there is a measurable, non-random difference in elderly population percentages across the different density groups. In the linear regression without Flevoland, the relationship between density and aging was also significant (p 0.014), showing that density explains a substantial part of the spatial variation in aging.

What do the results imply/suggest?

The results suggest that population aging in the Netherlands is not spatially even, and that population density is an important factor. Less dense, more rural provinces tend to have higher shares of elderly residents, while denser areas are relatively younger, with Flevoland standing out as a special dense-but-young case. This implies that low-density regions, especially in the south and east, may face stronger pressures on healthcare, labor markets, and local services as their populations continue to age.

What might you do differently next time?

Next time, I would try to include additional spatial factors that might explain aging, such as migration patterns, housing characteristics, or access to services, rather than just density alone.
